What is a shark?

Sharks, belonging to the superorder Selachimorpha, are remarkable cartilaginous fish renowned for their streamlined bodies, sharp teeth, and predatory prowess. Adapted to diverse marine environments, they've evolved into a multitude of species, contributing to the ocean's biodiversity. With their cartilaginous skeletons, dorsal fins, and powerful tails, sharks play a vital role as apex predators, regulating marine ecosystems and maintaining balance. Despite their importance, sharks face threats such as overfishing and habitat loss, necessitating urgent conservation measures to protect these extraordinary creatures and preserve the health of our oceans.

Sharks are important because...

Sharks play a vital role in marine ecosystems as apex predators, regulating prey populations and maintaining balance. Beyond their ecological significance, they contribute to scientific research, providing insights into ocean health. Sharks also hold cultural and economic value, supporting tourism and coastal communities. Protecting sharks is essential for preserving ocean life and fostering sustainable coexistence with human activities.

Types of Sharks

Great White Shark
Known for its large size and powerful predatory behavior. Found in coastal waters around the world.
Hammerhead Shark
Recognized for its distinctive hammer-shaped head. Various species, including the Great Hammerhead and Scalloped Hammerhead.
Tiger Shark
Named for its dark stripes resembling a tiger's pattern. Often found in tropical and temperate waters.
Whale Shark
The largest known extant fish species. Filter feeder, feeding on plankton and small fish. Found in tropical and warm-temperate seas.
Mako Shark
Known for its incredible speed and agility. Shortfin Mako and Longfin Mako are the two main species.
Bull Shark
Able to tolerate freshwater and often found in rivers and estuaries. Known for its aggressive behavior.
